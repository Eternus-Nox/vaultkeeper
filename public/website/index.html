01 / WHY IT MATTERS

The way we handle
passwords is broken.

The average person has more than a hundred online accounts. Nobody can remember a strong, unique password for every one of them — so we don't. We reuse, we shortcut, we hope. And that's exactly what attackers count on.

— THE PROBLEM
100+

Accounts per person.

Banking, work, shopping, every "create an account to view" paywall, every newsletter signup. The number keeps growing. Nobody is built to remember that many strong, unique secrets.

— THE SHORTCUT
2/3

People reuse passwords.

Two out of three people use the same password — or small variations of it — across multiple accounts. It's the convenience-or-security tradeoff most of us quietly lose every day.

— THE FALLOUT
14B

Passwords already leaked.

Fourteen billion username-and-password pairs are sitting in public breach databases right now. If you've used the internet for a while, statistically — yours are in there too.

— WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS

One leaked site becomes a hundred breaches.

When a website you signed up for years ago gets breached, your email and password get added to a public list. Attackers run automated tools that take that list and try those same username-and-password pairs against thousands of other sites — your bank, your email, your work account, your cloud photos. If you reused that password anywhere, every one of those accounts can fall in a single afternoon.

Yahoo. LinkedIn. Adobe. Dropbox. T-Mobile. Marriott. Every breach you've ever shrugged off as "not my problem" became ammunition. Somewhere on the internet, your email address is sitting next to a list of every password you've ever typed on a leaky site. It's already happened. The only question is what you do about it now.

— 01

Memory doesn't scale.

Even a strong password becomes a weak one the moment you reuse it. One breach exposes every account that shares it. And there's no way to remember a hundred unique strong passwords without help.

— 02

Browsers aren't safe enough.

The "save password" feature in Chrome, Edge, Safari, and Firefox is the first thing modern malware looks for. A single bad download can hand over every password your browser has ever saved.

— 03

2FA helps, but isn't enough.

Two-factor authentication is a strong second layer, but it's only as good as the password under it. Many sites still don't offer it, and SMS codes can be intercepted. The password is still the foundation.

The fix is simple in concept: a different long, random password for every account, and something trustworthy that remembers them for you. The hard part is finding something you can actually trust to hold them — which is where most password managers quietly fall short.

03 / THE PROMISE

Three things we cannot
do — by design.

Trust isn't a marketing bullet. It's an architectural choice we made early and won't walk back.

— 01

We can't read your data.

Your vault is encrypted with a key derived from your master password — a key we never possess and never see. To us, your passwords are a string of meaningless characters. There is no backdoor, because there is no door.

— 02

We can't track you.

No analytics. No telemetry. No "anonymized usage data." We don't know which sites you save, which devices you use, or how often you log in. Not because we promise we won't — because we never built the wires to do it.

— 03

We can't recover your account.

If you forget your master password, no support agent can help you. No "trusted device" trick. No backup unlock. The same architecture that keeps attackers out keeps everyone out — including us. Choose your master password carefully.

Major password managers have been breached. When the encryption model is "trust the company," compromise of the company is compromise of you.

07 / FAQ

A few questions answered.

How is "true zero-knowledge" different from what other password managers claim?

Most password managers say "zero-knowledge." Some mean it. Some add caveats — analytics that track which sites you visit, telemetry that phones home about your devices, recovery features that let employees re-enable access. VaultKeeper has none of those wires running in the background. Your master password stays on your device, your data is encrypted before it leaves, and we collect zero behavioral information. There's no trust us — we built it so you don't have to.

What happens if I forget my master password?

Nothing recovers it. Your master password is the foundation of your entire vault — there is no admin override, no recovery email path, no support ticket that can decrypt your data. This is the tradeoff for true privacy: nobody can recover your data because nobody else can read it. Choose a strong master password and consider keeping a paper backup of it somewhere safe.

Will it work on my iPhone? Android? Computer?

Yes — VaultKeeper is a progressive web app, so it runs in any modern browser and installs to your home screen on iOS, Android, macOS, and Windows. The same vault, the same experience, everywhere you go.

If your servers got hacked, what would the attacker see?

A pile of meaningless characters. Every vault is encrypted with a key derived from the user's master password — a key the server never has. With our entire database, an attacker would still need to break every individual user's master password to access anything. That's the difference: a breach of us isn't a breach of you.

Can my family members use it too?

Yes. Each user gets their own independent vault — they can't see each other's data, and neither can anyone else. Sign-up is open by default so the people you invite can simply create an account and start using their own vault right away.

What about importing from another password manager?

VaultKeeper imports from Bitwarden, 1Password 8, LastPass, Enpass, and any generic CSV format. The import happens locally on your device, so your existing passwords are encrypted before they're saved. Most migrations take less than five minutes.

Can I export my data if I want to leave?

Always. Export your full vault as decrypted JSON for safekeeping or as an encrypted archive for portable migration. You're never locked in.
